Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q: What is the difference between a simulation and a digital twin?

A: Think of a simulation as a "what-if" experiment; it's a virtual model built to predict how a design might behave under specific conditions. A digital twin, however, is a living replica that stays connected to its physical counterpart through real-time sensors and data. While a simulation helps you design a car, a digital twin monitors that specific car as you drive it down the road. The biggest difference is connectivity: simulations are static and manual, while digital twins evolve instantly with real-world changes. One is used to study general possibilities, while the other provides a constant pulse on a specific, existing asset. Essentially, a simulation is a rehearsal, whereas a digital twin is a live performance that adjusts in real time.

Q: How much does it cost to implement a digital twin?

A: Building a digital twin is a bit like buying a car; you can get a reliable "base model" pilot for around $50,000, but a high-performance "luxury" system for a factory often clears $1 million. The initial sticker price is just the start, as the real work involves the "under the hood" labor of wiring your old equipment and data into the new software. You also have to factor in monthly "fuel" costs like cloud hosting and security, which can range from a few thousand to $30,000 depending on how much data you're crunching. Ultimately, most people find that after five years of updates and hiring experts to run it, they've spent about three times what they originally planned.

Q: Which industries use digital twins the most?

A: Digital twins are most common in industries where mistakes are too expensive or dangerous to risk. Car manufacturers use them to design vehicles and simulate crashes, while aerospace companies use virtual engines to ensure flight safety. In manufacturing, these digital clones monitor factory robots to prevent breakdowns before they happen. Energy providers rely on them to manage massive power grids and predict when wind turbines need maintenance. Healthcare is also adopting the tech, allowing doctors to practice surgeries on virtual patient models first. Even city planners use them to sync traffic lights and improve urban flow. Essentially, any sector dealing with complex systems uses them to save time, money, and lives.
